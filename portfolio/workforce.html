the problem

Currently, WorkForce is not optimized for easy of use or user's enjoyment. Applying for unemployment benefits is an over-complicated, disorganized, and stressful process, but a redesign could transform the process of filing for unemployment. Therefore, WorkForce needs a user-friendly, productive website that will get them through this daunting process more easily.

methods and timeline

Week 1: Beginning the Design Journey

September 26th - October 2nd, 2022

We jumped in with our design strategy--our focus for our redesign, some initial issues with WorkForce's website, and the most important aspect: our users. This included user personas, mind mapping, a user journey map, and our user problem statement. From here, we began wireframing and drafting an initial user testing plan.

mind map in Miro

user journey map

low-fidelity wireframes in Figma

Week 2: Evaluating the Current WorkForce Website

October 3rd - October 9th, 2022

To evaluate Workforce's website, we used a few methods:

  • content inventory audit
  • content heirarchy evaluation
  • information architecture and navigation system analysis
  • and sitemapping

With this, we were able to indentify WorkForce's main issues. This includes an oversaturated color palette, a confusing layout, and web pages that are difficult to read, however their most glaring issues include a lack of customer support and a confusing application process.

annotations of the WorkForce homepage

From here, we began our redesign of WorkForce's navigation system, including buttons and their main navigation menu.

navigation system in Figma

Week 3: Design Sprints and Wireframing

October 10th - October 16th, 2022

After evaluating the WorkForce website, we were able to start iterating our designs using the principles of design we had just learned in class.


We began with design sprints to iterate our low-fidelity wireframes from Week 1 into mid-fidelity wireframes. We also started our first pattern library to make our designs more consistent. From here, we began connecting our wireframes into prototypes.

mid-fidelity wireframes in Figma

Week 4: Responsive Design

October 17th - October 23rd, 2022

As we continued the design process, we started using grids to align our designs. We also began adapting our desktop designs for mobile as we learned about responsive design and mobile-first design in class.

mobile homepage

jellybean, the northern cardinal

West Virginia's state bird is a northern cardinal, so we wanted to include that in our design. This version was drawn by our project manager, Patty, who also named it Jellybean.

For an A/B test, we tested Jellybean's icon. We found that it gave the website more of a playful feel, and users appreciated having it around. This link provides more results.

Week 5: Finalizing our Designs

October 24th - October 30th, 2022

This week, we were rapidly approaching the end of our project. To finalize our designs, we completed our final style guide, and finished our high-fidelity prototypes.

high-fidelity wireframes in Figma

As mentioned in our style guide, we wanted Workforce to feel friendly, approachable, and calming. By including photos of West Virginia in the website, we also wanted our users to feel more at home on our site, hopefully also working to shift some misconceptions about unemployment insurance.

Lastly, we completed our final round of user testing and reflected on further iterations we could complete if given the time.

conclusion

what I learned
  • the importance of note-taking through every step of the process
  • the importance of files and naming conventions
  • research--we found that the rates of drug overdose in the state of West Virginia are nearly triple that of the national rate since the beginning of covid. because of this, we added a banner to our redesign with a suicide hotline, however it would have been helpful speaking to actual West Virginia residents about what they wanted out of an unemployment website as well as general opinions on applying for unemployment in West Virginia (is it common? is it looked down on? or is it not even talked about becuase it's too confusing to figure out?)
  • research fonts and colors typically used in unemployment websites--what do people find trustworthy and calming on government websites? what's not trustworthy? what creates a stressful environment on a website?
  • what i'd do differently next time: spend more time in the empathy and research stage--what are users actually looking for? because this was a project focused on the visual design layer of a website and primarily updating the user interface, we didn't work as much on the user experience, but i would have liked to have spent more time on that aspect to get a better idea of what users want and need out of an unemployment website
